About This Tool

This tool was built to help biostatistics students develop an intuitive understanding of linear regression — not just how to run the test, but what the numbers actually mean and how to critically evaluate whether the model is appropriate. All statistics are computed live from real biological datasets, so every chart, equation, and p-value reflects the data you're looking at. No software installation required — runs entirely in your browser.

Intended Audience

Undergraduate and graduate students in introductory biostatistics, ecology, or biological research methods courses. Designed for students who have been introduced to the concept of linear regression and want to build a deeper visual and quantitative understanding before applying it to their own data.

How to Read a Residual Plot

You must always examine the residual plot before trusting any linear regression — even when R² looks impressive. R² can be high while the model is fundamentally wrong. The residual plot reveals what R² hides.

✓ Random scatter — model OK
Points scattered randomly above and below zero; equal spread throughout. Linear model is appropriate — proceed.
✗ Curved pattern — non-linearity
Systematic curve = the true relationship is non-linear. Try log, square-root, or polynomial transformation of x or y.
✗ Fan shape — heteroscedasticity
Spread increases with fitted value — variance is not constant. Log-transforming y often corrects this in biological data.
✗ Outlier or high-leverage point
One extreme point can distort the entire line. Investigate: data error, measurement issue, or a genuine biological anomaly?
The golden rule: Always plot your residuals. A high R² with a curved residual pattern means your model is wrong — it is capturing variance, but for the wrong mathematical reason. No single statistic replaces this visual check. The Fish Allometry dataset in this tool demonstrates exactly this problem.

What is a residual?
A residual is the difference between the observed value (y) and the predicted value (ŷ) from the regression line: e = y − ŷ. Positive = above the line; negative = below.
What does R² mean?
R² is the proportion of total variation in y explained by x. R² = 0.85 means 85% of variance is accounted for. A high R² does NOT guarantee a linear model is appropriate — always check the residual plot first.
SST = SSR + SSE
Total variation (SST) = variation explained by regression (SSR) + leftover variation (SSE). R² = SSR/SST. Click any point on the scatter plot to see this decomposition drawn geometrically.
The p-value for slope
Tests H₀: slope = 0. A small p-value (< 0.05) means strong evidence of a linear association in the population, not just in your sample. Combine with R² and the residual plot for a full picture.
